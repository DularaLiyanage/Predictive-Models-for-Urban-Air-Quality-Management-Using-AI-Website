📚 1. Literature Survey

Previous research on air quality prediction has explored a wide range of machine learning and deep learning approaches. Statistical models such as ARIMA and SARIMA were among the earliest used for time-series forecasting of pollutant concentrations, though their linear assumptions limited accuracy with highly non-linear urban pollution data.

More recent studies have demonstrated strong results with LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) neural networks, which effectively capture long-range temporal dependencies in sequential sensor data. XGBoost and ensemble tree methods have shown competitive performance, particularly for tabular feature-rich datasets incorporating weather variables such as wind speed, humidity, and temperature.

IoT-based sensor deployments for low-cost air quality monitoring have been studied in cities across South and South-East Asia; however, Sri Lanka remains comparatively underrepresented in the literature. Platforms such as AirVisual and PurpleAir demonstrate the viability of citizen-science sensor networks, while academic efforts increasingly combine sensor fusion with geo-spatial interpolation to fill coverage gaps.

🔍 2. Research Gap

Existing systems and published research exhibit the following key limitations:

  • Existing systems lack real-time adaptability — predictions are batch-based and not continuously updated with incoming sensor streams.
  • Most solutions offer coarse spatial resolution, providing city-level AQI rather than neighbourhood or micro-zone granularity.
  • There is a severe lack of localised models trained on Sri Lankan meteorological and traffic patterns, limiting the applicability of imported models.
  • Available commercial platforms do not provide actionable health advisory outputs tailored to local demographics and vulnerability profiles.
  • Integration of predictive forecasting with real-time IoT streams in a unified, low-latency pipeline remains absent in published Sri Lankan literature.

❓ 3. Research Problem

Urban areas in Sri Lanka — particularly Colombo, Kandy, Negombo, and Galle — are experiencing rapidly deteriorating air quality driven by vehicle emissions, industrial activity, and construction. Despite this, cities lack intelligent, data-driven systems capable of monitoring pollution levels in real time and providing reliable short-term forecasts to enable preemptive action by individuals, health services, and municipal authorities.

The absence of such a system means that residents are exposed to hazardous air quality events without warning, and policymakers cannot evaluate the spatiotemporal impact of interventions. This research addresses the gap by designing and validating an end-to-end AI-powered monitoring and forecasting platform tuned to Sri Lanka's unique urban conditions.

🎯 4. Research Objectives

This project is guided by four primary objectives:

🧠

Develop AI Forecasting Model

Design, train, and evaluate LSTM and XGBoost models to accurately predict AQI up to 24 hours in advance using historical and live sensor data.

📡

Build IoT Data Collection System

Assemble and deploy low-cost sensor nodes measuring PM2.5, PM10, CO₂, NO₂, temperature, and humidity, transmitting data via MQTT in real time.

🔔

Create Smart Alert System

Implement automated, threshold-based and ML-triggered push notifications to warn citizens and authorities of approaching hazardous AQI levels.

📱

Develop User Dashboard

Build a cross-platform Flutter mobile application providing interactive AQI maps, historical trends, forecasts, and personalised health recommendations.

⚙️ 5. Methodology

The system follows a six-stage data pipeline from physical sensing to end-user output:

🔌 IoT Sensors
→
📡 MQTT Broker
→
🧹 Pre-processing
→
🧠 ML Models
→
⚡ FastAPI Backend
→
📱 Flutter App

Stage 1 — Data Collection: IoT sensor nodes (ESP32 + SDS011 + MQ-135) are deployed at key urban locations. Readings are published at 60-second intervals to an MQTT broker hosted on a cloud VM.

Stage 2 — Ingestion & Storage: A Python subscriber service consumes MQTT messages, applies schema validation, and persists time-series records to MongoDB collections.

Stage 3 — Pre-processing: Missing value imputation (KNN interpolation), outlier removal (IQR filter), feature engineering (lag features, rolling statistics), and min-max normalisation prepare data for modelling.

Stage 4 — Model Training & Evaluation: LSTM and XGBoost models are trained on labelled historical datasets. Performance is measured using RMSE, MAE, and R². The best model per sensor cluster is persisted via MLflow.

Stage 5 — API Layer: FastAPI exposes RESTful endpoints for current readings, forecast retrieval, and alert subscriptions. Endpoints are secured with JWT authentication.

Stage 6 — Frontend: A Flutter mobile app renders real-time AQI maps (Google Maps SDK), time-series charts (fl_chart), and push notifications (FCM) for Android and iOS.
