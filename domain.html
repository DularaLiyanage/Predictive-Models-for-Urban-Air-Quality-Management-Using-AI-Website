📚 1. Literature Survey

A. Time-Series Forecasting of Urban Air Pollution

Recent Sri Lankan studies have shown the potential of data-driven forecasting using models like LSTM and XGBoost to predict individual pollutants such as PM2.5 and PM10. However, these models are limited by their single-pollutant scope and lack of model transparency. Global research indicates that hybrid frameworks combining linear statistical methods (ARIMA) with deep learning non-linearity significantly outperform standalone models in handling complex air quality dynamics. Despite recent advancements, the use of Transformer-based hybrid models for forecasting multiple pollutants simultaneously remains unexplored locally.

B. Spatial Estimation of Urban Air Pollution

Urban air pollution estimation has evolved from traditional geostatistical methods to machine learning and deep learning frameworks. Deep learning models capture complex spatial and temporal dependencies but require significant computing power and large training datasets. Classical methods like regression, random forest, and kriging are interpretable and efficient but struggle in high-dimensional, rapidly changing urban environments. Few studies have combined these approaches into a single unified framework — a gap this research addresses.

C. Time-Based Pollution Risk Scoring & Alert Simulation

Accurate forecasting of non-linear air quality data is greatly enhanced by CEEMDAN decomposition, which separates chaotic noise from steady diurnal and seasonal trends to stabilise 24-hour predictions. Integration of HYSPLIT trajectory analysis validates transboundary pollution contributions. Existing models handle meteorological features separately and lack systems for converting complex interactions into public health alerts — particularly given the negative correlation between PM2.5 and factors such as temperature and wind speed.

D. Air Quality Anomaly Detection and Pollution Event Characterisation

Atmospheric anomalies are early warning signs of poor air quality. Current research underscores the influence of temporal-calendar factors on pollution spikes. Learning both pointwise representations and event-wise associations is essential for understanding these spikes. Integrating temporal calendar data with deep learning models like GRU or LSTM, enhanced with XAI, provides a clear path for predicting and explaining environmental anomalies for public health applications.

🔍 2. Research Gap

Despite global advances, a significant gap exists in Sri Lankan and broader developing-country contexts:

  • The CEA operates just two fixed automated monitoring stations with a few low-cost sensors, resulting in large spatial coverage gaps.
  • Existing local systems take a reactive approach — analysing past data — rather than using forecasting for prevention.
  • Current research focuses on single-pollutant models without interpretability tools, limiting their policy utility.
  • There is a lack of an integrated, multi-pollutant forecasting system combining high predictive accuracy with spatial scalability and model transparency.
  • No publicly accessible system in Sri Lanka translates pollution data into actionable health recommendations and automated alerts for citizens.

❓ 3. Research Problem

Air pollution has become a major environmental and public health concern in Sri Lanka. Globally, air pollution causes approximately 7 million deaths annually. In the South-East Asia and Western Pacific regions alone, 2.2 million and 2.8 million pollution-related deaths are recorded respectively. Sri Lanka is witnessing increasing air quality problems driven by rapid urbanisation and vehicular emissions in cities like Colombo, Kandy, and Kurunegala.

The CEA's minimal monitoring infrastructure results in a large spatial coverage gap, forcing a purely reactive approach to pollution management. The absence of an integrated, intelligent system means residents face hazardous air quality events without warning, and policymakers cannot evaluate the spatiotemporal impact of interventions. This research addresses the gap by designing a modular, explainable AI framework validated on CEA data from 2019–2024.

🎯 4. Research Objectives

🔮

Hybrid Multi-Pollutant Forecasting

Develop a SARIMAX–Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) model capturing both linear and non-linear volatilities for PM2.5, PM10, NO₂, SO₂, and O₃ forecasting.

🗺️

Spatial Scalability

Implement an Ensemble Spatial Estimation framework using geospatial datasets (SRTM, ESA WorldCover, WorldPop, OSM) to generate accurate pollution maps for unmonitored regions.

🚨

Proactive Risk Assessment

Integrate a CEEMDAN–Seq2Seq model to forecast 24-hour AQI trends and trigger automated health alerts using CEA AQI breakpoints.

🔍

Anomaly Detection & XAI

Deploy an ensemble-based GRU framework to identify abrupt pollution spikes over a 7-day horizon, with XGBoost + SHAP for causal transparency and preventive health tips.

⚙️ 5. Methodology

A. Time-Series Forecasting — SARIMAX–TFT Hybrid

Historical data (2019–2024) from the CEA was imputed using Seasonality-Aware Imputation (Linear Interpolation, MICE). Features include wind vectors, binary monsoon activity flags, and traffic peak indicators. The pollutant concentration Yₜ is decomposed into a log-transformed SARIMAX linear baseline (L̂ₜ) and a TFT residual learner (R̂TFT):

eₜ = Yₜ − L̂ₜ    →    Ŷfinal = exp(L̂ₜ) + R̂TFT

A ReLU function ensures non-negative outputs. XAI attention weights quantify feature importance and outputs are mapped to CEA AQI standards.

B. Ensemble Spatial Estimation

LSTM networks forecast one-hour-ahead pollutant concentrations at Battaramulla and Kandy stations (80/10/10 train/val/test split). Predictions are fed into an ensemble spatial estimator on a 1 km × 1 km grid using fixed weighted averaging:

RBF — 0.25 (smooth interpolation) GP — 0.25 (uncertainty-aware) KNN — 0.20 (local similarity) IDW — 0.30 (distance baseline)

Auxiliary geospatial datasets: elevation (SRTM), land cover (ESA WorldCover), population density (WorldPop), and road networks (OpenStreetMap). Results visualised via Flutter web heatmaps with FastAPI back-end.

C. Time-Based Pollution Risk Scoring

Raw pollutant concentrations are converted to standardised AQI time series per CEA breakpoints. CEEMDAN decomposes non-stationary AQI signals into stable Intrinsic Mode Functions (IMFs). A Seq2Seq-Attention encoder-decoder integrates CEEMDAN outputs with a Transboundary Risk Index (HYSPLIT backward trajectories) and a heuristic Traffic Risk Score to forecast 24-hour AQI. Forecasts are displayed as 90% confidence intervals with CEA-classified risk levels and automated alert triggering. Permutation Feature Importance is used as the XAI layer.

D. Air Quality Anomaly Detection

Ensemble anomaly detection uses Isolation Forest, One-Class SVM, and traditional statistical methods with majority-vote logic (2/3 consensus) to flag high-confidence outliers. K-Means clustering categorises detected anomalies into event types by severity, duration, and calendar context. A GRU model (80/20 train-test split) forecasts anomalies over a 7-day horizon. XGBoost + SHAP explains causal drivers and outputs specific preventive health tips — bridging data science and public health intervention.
